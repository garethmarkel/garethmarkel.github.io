This paper is currently accepted and forthcoming in *American Economic Review: Insights*. AEA members can preview it [here](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aeri.20250281&&from=f), and the working paper can be found [on SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5225447).


#### Technologies and topics used: Big data, scientific computing, quantitative genetics, R, econometrics

At each location in the genome, humans have two alleles (think of this as a unit of genetic variation) of DNA. Each parent contributes one allele for a given location, which is randomly chosen from that parent's two alleles. This means that a child shares exactly 50% of their genotype with each parent. A secondary consequence is that this induces random variation in how genetically similar two non-MZ twin siblings are. While we often think of it as 50% sharing, it's only 50% on average--in practice, it's roughly normally distributed with a standard deviation of ~0.04 and a mean of ~0.5. We call this phenomenon identity-by-descent (IBD) sharing. Check out the distribution of genetic similarity for 20k pairs of siblings in the UK Biobank:

![](images/ibd_sharing.png)

This is cool on its own, but also quantitatively useful for some core questions in human genetics. Scientists of all backgrounds have long been interested in finding out how much of the variation in a given trait is due to genetics and how much is due to environment (heritability). However, we couldn't even really look at the human genome in detail until the 2000s, and had to find "workarounds." One workaround was to use sets of twins to study this question, since MZ and DZ twins have different expected levels of genetic relatedness, and could compare the similarity of thier outcomes to the (known) similarity of thier genomes. This kind of study helped show that schizophrenia is a genetic condition, and not caused by "cold mothers" (the leading theory of the early 1900s), but can be applied to almost any trait. 

However, this method has come under criticism due to the "equal environments assumption"--basically, if identical twins have a similar environment that disprportionately makes thier outcomes more similar than a pair of nonidentical twins outcomes would be, then any estimates of the genetic effect on that trait variation will be biased. This is where IBD sharing comes in. This is an incredibly clean natural experiment--the degree of genetic similarity between two siblings is randomly assigned at conception. This means we can directly regress the similarity between the outcomes of two siblings, on thier degree of similarity, and identify the effect of genetics on the variance of a trait without assumptions like the EEA. The figure below illustrates exactly how this works--the equation being shown is $Y_1 Y_2 = \hat{\alpha_0} + \hat{\alpha_1}\pi$ for a pair of siblings.

![](images/ibdreg.png)

One drawback of this design is you need *many* pairs of siblings to be well powered. My coauthors and I used data on over 1 million genotyped study participants (who hailed from 6 countries and 2 continents) to identify ~80k pairs of genotyped siblings from over. Using this large sample, we computed heritability estimates for 15 traits, ranging from social science outcomes like self-reported wellbeing, to healthcare outcomes like smoking status and fertility.
